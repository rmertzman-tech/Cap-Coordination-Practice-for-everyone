Capability-Based Coordination in Practice: Lessons from Multi-Stakeholder Networks

Author: Robert Mertzman, A.M. (Philosophy), C. Ph. (History and Philosophy of Science)

Institution: Ethics Institute, Saint Petersburg College

Target Journal: Journal of Applied Philosophy

Abstract

Multi-stakeholder coordination around complex ethical problems often fails when diverse groups cannot work together effectively despite shared concerns. Building on the capability-based coordination framework developed in "Capability-Based Coordination and the Bootstrap Resolution of Normative Authority" (Mertzman, under review at Synthese), this paper demonstrates how network goals and bootstrap authority operate in practice across real-world coordination challenges.

Through systematic cross-case analysis of coordination transformations—from the NFL's CTE crisis to Dutch flood management, platform cooperatives, and environmental movements—I show that successful coordination emerges when stakeholders unite around genuine network goals: collective objectives that different approaches can advance equivalently while preserving stakeholder authenticity. The cases reveal universal patterns across dramatically different contexts, time periods, and cultural settings: diverse stakeholders develop coordination competence through sustained engagement with shared objectives, gaining normative authority through demonstrated effectiveness rather than formal credentials or expert knowledge.

The primary contributions are practical implementation tools for building coordination networks: diagnostic frameworks for distinguishing genuine network goals from pseudo-coordination that serves dominant interests, stakeholder mapping approaches across knowledge-connection-resource-authority domains, and systematic guidance for developing coordination competence accessible to ordinary people confronting systematic problems. Cross-case validation demonstrates these tools work across contexts from medieval water management to contemporary digital platforms, making effective ethical action accessible through multiple contribution pathways—leading, following, supporting, connecting, amplifying, or implementing.

This applied framework complements the theoretical foundations by translating capability-based coordination principles into concrete guidance for democratic participation in addressing systematic ethical challenges. The analysis demonstrates that anyone can develop coordination competence around genuinely shared objectives, democratizing ethical authority through contribution-based participation rather than requiring specialized expertise, and providing practical tools for building effective networks around complex problems requiring collective action while respecting individual authenticity and cultural difference.

Keywords: applied ethics, stakeholder coordination, normative authority, network governance, democratic participation, capability-based coordination

I. Introduction: A Crisis of Coordination and Democratic Promise

When forensic pathologist Bennet Omalu called the Pittsburgh Steelers' team doctor in 2002 to discuss his autopsy of former center Mike Webster, he encountered more than medical skepticism. Webster—a Hall of Fame player who had anchored four Super Bowl victories—died at fifty after years of cognitive decline, homelessness, and self-destructive behavior that his family attributed to repeated head trauma from two decades of professional football. But when Omalu's examination revealed extensive chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE), a degenerative brain disease linked to repetitive head impacts (Omalu et al., 2005), the National Football League's response was swift and uncompromising: demand retraction, dismiss the methodology, and apply institutional pressure to silence inconvenient research.

This reaction revealed a coordination crisis that would test whether ordinary people could challenge one of America's most powerful entertainment institutions. The NFL's approach represented a familiar pattern: when systematic problems threaten institutional interests, those with formal authority often pursue individual organizational goals that exclude other stakeholders from meaningful participation. The league's Mild Traumatic Brain Injury committee, led by team physicians, focused on protecting organizational liability and maintaining revenue streams rather than establishing truth about brain injury or preventing future harm (Fainaru & Fainaru-Wada, 2013).

For nearly a decade, this monopolistic approach appeared successful. The NFL possessed the resources to fund research, the platform to shape public discourse, and the institutional authority to set player safety policies. Independent researchers faced funding difficulties, affected families lacked medical credibility, and journalists encountered access restrictions. The system seemed designed to ensure that those with the most authentic concern about player welfare—the players and families experiencing brain injury consequences—had the least influence over policies meant to protect them.

Yet by 2016, the same institution that had categorically denied any connection between football and brain disease was implementing comprehensive concussion protocols, funding independent research, and publicly acknowledging the relationship between repetitive head impacts and long-term neurological damage. This transformation didn't occur through regulatory intervention, legal mandate, or corporate social responsibility initiatives. Instead, it emerged from a coordination network that included brain injury researchers, affected families, investigative journalists, and legal advocates who discovered they could advance the same shared objective—establishing truth about CTE and preventing future harm—through their distinctive but equivalent contributions.

Beyond Expert Authority: The Democratic Promise of Coordination Networks

What makes this transformation philosophically significant extends far beyond sports policy. The NFL case demonstrates that effective ethical action can emerge when diverse stakeholders unite around genuine network goals—collective objectives that different approaches can advance equivalently while preserving stakeholder authenticity. This challenges dominant assumptions about how systematic ethical problems get addressed and who has the authority to participate meaningfully in solutions.

Traditional approaches to multi-stakeholder coordination typically require either content agreement (everyone must share beliefs and values), procedural compromise (everyone must moderate their positions), hierarchical authority (experts or officials make decisions for others), or market mechanisms (economic incentives align individual interests). Each approach has significant limitations: content agreement eliminates diversity, compromise reduces effectiveness, hierarchy excludes stakeholders, and market mechanisms treat ethical coordination as economic transaction.

The coordination network that transformed NFL policy succeeded through a different mechanism: capability equivalence around shared network goals. Families contributed experiential knowledge and moral authority through testimony about player decline; scientists contributed research methodology and peer-reviewed evidence through brain banking and analysis; lawyers contributed procedural accountability through litigation and discovery; journalists contributed transparency and public education through investigative reporting. These different approaches enhanced rather than competed with each other because they advanced the same collective objective through functionally equivalent but distinctive contributions.

This pattern reveals possibilities for democratizing ethical authority by showing that coordination competence—rather than formal credentials, institutional position, or specialized expertise—can create legitimate influence over systematic problems. NFL families gained authority not through medical training but through demonstrated capability for advancing brain injury research and prevention goals that others came to value and rely upon. Their questions became compelling, their engagement became essential, and their voices became influential because they consistently contributed to shared objectives that transcended individual purposes.

The democratic promise lies in recognizing that anyone confronting systematic ethical problems can potentially develop similar coordination competence through authentic engagement with shared objectives. This doesn't require abandoning professional expertise or institutional authority, but rather recognizing that effective coordination often emerges when diverse types of knowledge and capability contribute equivalently to collective goals that no single approach can achieve alone.

Types of Network Contributions: Making Coordination Accessible

The NFL transformation succeeded because it recognized that effective coordination requires diverse types of contributions rather than uniform expertise or leadership capability. This insight makes ethical action accessible by showing multiple ways people can participate meaningfully in addressing systematic problems:

  • Knowledge Contributors provide expertise, research, local experience, or specialized understanding that advances shared objectives.
  • Connection Contributors link different stakeholder groups, translate between communities, and facilitate communication across cultural or professional differences.
  • Resource Contributors offer funding, platform access, institutional support, or material resources that enable coordination activities.
  • Amplification Contributors handle communication, education, advocacy, or public engagement that extends coordination reach and impact.
  • Implementation Contributors focus on practical execution, monitoring, maintenance, or direct service that translates coordination into concrete outcomes.
  • Support Contributors provide sustained attention, documentation, encouragement, or logistical assistance that maintains coordination over time.

This framework demonstrates that coordination networks need distributed contributions rather than centralized leadership, making effective ethical action accessible to anyone willing to develop coordination capabilities around genuinely shared objectives.

Theoretical Contribution: Network Goals Enable Coordinated Authenticity

The NFL case illustrates a broader theoretical insight: how can diverse stakeholders coordinate effectively without requiring them to abandon their authentic values, approaches, or identities? Network-goal coordination offers an alternative by focusing on shared objectives that different approaches can advance equivalently rather than requiring methodological or values convergence. This creates what might be called "coordinated authenticity"—the ability to work together effectively while remaining true to diverse values, methods, and cultural commitments, specifically in service of collective goals rather than individual purposes.

This framework addresses limitations in existing coordination theory. Social movement theory struggles with professional integration (McAdam et al., 1996). Network governance theory typically coordinates across different objectives rather than focusing diverse approaches on the same shared goal (Ostrom, 2010). Public policy theory often treats stakeholder engagement as input to expert decision-making rather than equivalent contribution.

Network-goal coordination explains coordination persistence and cultural transferability. When stakeholders pursue genuine shared objectives, coordination persists through changing circumstances. When coordination focuses on functional capabilities, approaches transfer across different contexts.

Preview of Evidence: Universal Patterns Across Contexts

The NFL transformation represents one instance of coordination patterns that appear across dramatically different contexts. Dutch communities have coordinated flood management for over 800 years. Platform cooperatives like Stocksy demonstrate democratic digital coordination (Scholz, 2016). Wangari Maathai's Green Belt Movement shows how environmental restoration can scale from local to international levels (Berkes, 2007).

These cases share common principles: diverse stakeholders uniting around genuine network goals, bootstrap authority development through competence, and capability equivalence enabling different approaches to contribute equally. This suggests that network-goal coordination is a universal phenomenon for addressing systematic coordination challenges.

II. Pattern Recognition Across Contexts: Network Goals Enable Coordination

Successful coordination networks share a common structure: diverse stakeholders unite around genuine network goals that their different approaches can advance equivalently, while developing bootstrap authority through demonstrated coordination competence. This pattern appears across dramatically different contexts, revealing universal principles.

The NFL Transformation: From Monopolistic Control to Network Coordination

Initial Coordination Failure (2005-2010)

The NFL's initial approach failed because it pursued individual institutional goals (protecting liability, maintaining revenue) that other stakeholders couldn't advance equivalently. Families, scientists, legal professionals, and journalists were systematically excluded because their authentic goals conflicted with the league's.

Coordination Transformation (2010-2020)

Transformation occurred when stakeholders recognized a genuine network goal: "establishing CTE truth and preventing future harm." Each group could advance this through their authentic approaches:

  • Families' Authentic Contribution: Experiential documentation and moral witness.
  • Scientists' Authentic Contribution: Research methodology and peer-reviewed evidence.
  • Legal Professionals' Authentic Contribution: Procedural accountability and information discovery.
  • Journalists' Authentic Contribution: Public education and transparency.

Key Success Factors

Success was driven by enhancement rather than competition among approaches, a sustained focus on the collective objective, and the development of bootstrap authority through demonstrated competence.

Dutch Water Boards: 800 Years of Coordination Resilience

The Dutch Waterschappen provide evidence of network-goal coordination's temporal resilience. Since the 12th century, they have coordinated flood management across diverse groups.

Historical Development and Network Goal Evolution

The network goal of "regional flood protection" emerged because no single group (farmers, craftsmen, merchants, political authorities) possessed all necessary capabilities. The goal evolved to include economic development and, more recently, "sustainable water management and climate adaptation," always allowing for diverse but equivalent contributions.

Institutional Resilience Mechanisms

Resilience comes from bootstrap authority preservation (authority derived from competence), adaptive coordination methods, capability equivalence maintenance, and the authentication of genuine network goals that remain meaningful to all stakeholders across centuries.

Platform Cooperatives: Democratic Digital Alternatives

Worker-owned digital platforms like Stocksy and Up & Go show how stakeholders can unite around "democratic digital rights and equitable ownership."

Coordination Challenge and Network Goal Identification

Platform cooperatives succeeded by recognizing that workers, users, developers, and community advocates shared an authentic interest in democratic digital rights, a goal that enables diverse contributions.

Implementation Through Capability Equivalence

At Stocksy, worker-owners, technical developers, users, and the cooperative board all contribute equivalently to democratic platform governance through their distinct capabilities.

Economic Viability and Network Goal Advancement

Platform cooperatives prove that democratic governance enhances economic viability and generates innovations (e.g., transparent algorithms, profit-sharing) that corporate alternatives cannot achieve.

Green Belt Movement: Environmental Coordination Across Scales

Wangari Maathai's Green Belt Movement demonstrates how network-goal coordination can scale from village-level organizing to international policy influence.

Multi-Scale Network Goal Development

The initial goal of "environmental restoration and community empowerment" enabled contributions from rural women, technical experts, and educational institutions. It later evolved to include policy influence, incorporating NGOs and government agencies.

Bootstrap Authority and Democratic Environmental Leadership

Authority emerged through demonstrated environmental restoration competence, not formal credentials. The movement democratized environmental leadership and persisted through political resistance because the network goal remained meaningful to all stakeholders.

Cross-Context Pattern Recognition

These four cases reveal universal coordination principles.

Universal Network Goal Characteristics

Genuine network goals enable authentic stakeholder advancement, foster enhancement rather than competition, and show temporal persistence.

Universal Capability Equivalence Patterns

Different approaches achieve functional equivalence, diverse knowledge is integrated without homogenization, and coordination approaches are culturally transferable.

Universal Bootstrap Authority Development

In all cases, authority is competence-based, democratically accessible, and persistent because it is grounded in functional competence.

III. Implementation Framework: Building Coordination Networks

The observed patterns translate into practical tools for building effective response networks.

Initial Assessment: Can You Contribute to Addressing This Issue?

Before attempting coordination, assess your authentic connection, temporal integration capability, and sustained engagement preparation.

Contribution Capability Assessment

Inventory your knowledge, skills, and resources. Assess your contribution style (leadership, support, connection building).

Network Goal Potential Assessment

Assess if the issue requires coordination, if different approaches could be enhancing, and if an authentic shared goal can be identified.

Advanced Stakeholder Mapping: Finding Your Coordination Network

Map potential partners across different types of concern and capability.

Knowledge Contributors: Evidence and Expertise

Include professional researchers, investigative journalists, technical specialists, and holders of experiential, community, and historical knowledge.

Connection Contributors: Bridge-Building and Translation

Identify cross-community connectors, cultural translators, and those with platform and communication access.

Resource Contributors: Implementation and Support

Map those with material resources, implementation capabilities, and institutional support.

Existing Authority Holders: Working With, Around, or Against Power

Assess formal decision-makers and institutional power centers to identify potential allies, neutral parties, and opposition forces.

Developing Bootstrap Authority: Systematic Competence Building

Normative authority emerges through demonstrated coordination competence.

Evidence Assessment Capability Development

Learn to evaluate information quality, integrate multiple perspectives, and share information competently.

Sustained Engagement Capability Development

Sustain motivation by connecting to authentic values, maintaining goal clarity, and building relationships.

Value Creation Capability Development

Create reciprocal benefits by identifying goal alignments, sharing resources, and distributing credit.

Cross-Case Validation: How Implementation Tools Work Across Contexts

These tools have proven effective across the cases studied, demonstrating their universal applicability and adaptability.

IV. Limitations and Appropriate Applications

Understanding when the framework fails or is inappropriate is essential for responsible implementation.

When Genuine Network Goals Cannot Be Identified

The framework may be structurally impossible in cases of zero-sum resource conflicts, irreconcilable value conflicts, or when emergency response requirements demand immediate, hierarchical action.

Detailed Failure Analysis: When Network-Goal Coordination Goes Wrong

An environmental justice coalition failed because "environmental health" was a pseudo-network goal meaning different things to different stakeholders with genuine tradeoffs. A corporate stakeholder engagement initiative manipulated network-goal language to co-opt opposition while continuing harmful practices.

Potential Misuse Scenarios: How the Framework Could Cause Harm

The framework could be misused for power imbalance masking, co-optation of resistance movements, as a delay tactic for urgent problems, or for cultural appropriation and imposition.

Cultural Boundaries and Respect

Issues affecting Indigenous communities require respect for sovereignty and self-determination. Coordination should only occur when communities genuinely invite outside involvement.

When Alternative Approaches Are More Appropriate

Expert authority (for technical safety), democratic process (for value conflicts), market mechanisms (for resource allocation), and emergency response (for crisis management) are sometimes more appropriate.

Diagnostic Questions for Appropriate Application

Before applying, assess the possibility of a genuine shared objective, cultural appropriateness, urgency, and power dynamics.

V. Implications for Applied Ethics: Democratic Coordination and Ethical Authority

Network-goal coordination contributes to applied ethics by demonstrating how objective normative outcomes can emerge from diverse stakeholder coordination.

Democratizing Ethical Authority: Beyond Expert Domination

Network-goal coordination preserves stakeholder authenticity while enabling collective action, creating "coordinated authenticity." This overcomes the limitations of approaches based on content agreement, procedural compromise, hierarchical authority, or market mechanisms.

Theoretical Innovation: Capability-Based Rather Than Content-Based Coordination

The framework shows that effective democratic action can emerge without consensus on underlying values or methods, transcending traditional models of deliberative democracy, interest group pluralism, and technocratic governance.

Bootstrap Authority and Democratic Legitimacy

Ethical authority can emerge through coordination competence rather than formal credentials. This authority is legitimized through service to the network and creates positive feedback loops, strengthening coordination over time.

Cross-Domain Applications: Scaling Democratic Coordination

The principles transfer to other domains like healthcare coordination, climate action, and technology governance, offering models that can address democratic deficits in each.

Methodological Contributions to Applied Ethics

The framework provides methodological innovations for stakeholder inclusion without relativism, cultural sensitivity without paralysis, expertise integration without domination, and practical effectiveness without theoretical compromise.

Implications for Democratic Theory

The framework contributes to democratic theory by showing how participation can be effective without homogenization, how authority can emerge without formal credentials, and how democratic innovation and resilience can exist outside of formal institutions.

Challenges and Future Research Directions

Challenges requiring future research include network goal authentication, power dynamic management, understanding cultural transferability limits, scale dynamics, and temporal sustainability.

VI. Conclusion: Making Ethical Action Accessible

The transformation from the NFL's monopolistic control to distributed coordination networks demonstrates that effective ethical action emerges when diverse stakeholders unite around genuine network goals.

Core Innovation: Network Goals Enable Democratic Coordination

The framework's central contribution is distinguishing genuine shared network objectives from individual interests. Network-goal coordination creates sustainability because diverse approaches enhance rather than compete with each other when focused on collective goals.

Democratic Promise: Authority Through Competence, Not Credentials

Ethical authority emerges through coordination competence. This democratization of authority appears across all successful cases, making effective ethical action accessible to ordinary people.

Practical Contribution: Tools for Building Coordination Networks

The framework provides concrete diagnostic tools, stakeholder mapping approaches, and guidance for developing bootstrap authority, making the transition from ethical insight to ethical action more accessible.

Theoretical Significance: Coordinated Authenticity for Collective Flourishing

Network-goal coordination contributes to applied ethics by showing how "coordinated authenticity" can emerge, bridging individual and collective ethics without requiring sacrifice or uniformity.

The Vision: Making Effective Ethical Action Accessible

Network-goal coordination demonstrates that human diversity becomes a resource for creating more robust collective action. It shows that ethical leadership belongs not to a professional class but to anyone willing to develop coordination competence around shared objectives. The evidence suggests that network-goal coordination represents a genuine phenomenon with universal applicability, offering possibilities for building more democratic, inclusive, and effective approaches to the systematic ethical problems we all face.
