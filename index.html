Capability-Based Coordination in Practice: Lessons from Multi-Stakeholder Networks

Author: Robert Mertzman, A.M. (Philosophy), C. Ph. (History and Philosophy of Science)
Institution: Ethics Institute, Saint Petersburg College
Target Journal: Journal of Applied Philosophy

Abstract

Multi-stakeholder coordination around complex ethical problems often fails when diverse groups cannot work together effectively despite shared concerns. Building on the capability-based coordination framework developed in "Capability-Based Coordination and the Bootstrap Resolution of Normative Authority" (Mertzman, under review at Synthese), this paper demonstrates how network goals and bootstrap authority operate in practice across real-world coordination challenges.

Through systematic cross-case analysis of coordination transformations—from the NFL's CTE crisis to Dutch flood management, platform cooperatives, and environmental movements—I show that successful coordination emerges when stakeholders unite around genuine network goals: collective objectives that different approaches can advance equivalently while preserving stakeholder authenticity. The cases reveal universal patterns across dramatically different contexts, time periods, and cultural settings: diverse stakeholders develop coordination competence through sustained engagement with shared objectives, gaining normative authority through demonstrated effectiveness rather than formal credentials or expert knowledge.

The primary contributions are practical implementation tools for building coordination networks: diagnostic frameworks for distinguishing genuine network goals from pseudo-coordination that serves dominant interests, stakeholder mapping approaches across knowledge-connection-resource-authority domains, and systematic guidance for developing coordination competence accessible to ordinary people confronting systematic problems. Cross-case validation demonstrates these tools work across contexts from medieval water management to contemporary digital platforms, making effective ethical action accessible through multiple contribution pathways—leading, following, supporting, connecting, amplifying, or implementing.

This applied framework complements the theoretical foundations by translating capability-based coordination principles into concrete guidance for democratic participation in addressing systematic ethical challenges. The analysis demonstrates that anyone can develop coordination competence around genuinely shared objectives, democratizing ethical authority through contribution-based participation rather than requiring specialized expertise, and providing practical tools for building effective networks around complex problems requiring collective action while respecting individual authenticity and cultural difference.

Keywords: applied ethics, stakeholder coordination, normative authority, network governance, democratic participation, capability-based coordination

I. Introduction: A Crisis of Coordination and Democratic Promise

When forensic pathologist Bennet Omalu called the Pittsburgh Steelers' team doctor in 2002 to discuss his autopsy of former center Mike Webster, he encountered more than medical skepticism. Webster—a Hall of Fame player who had anchored four Super Bowl victories—died at fifty after years of cognitive decline, homelessness, and self-destructive behavior that his family attributed to repeated head trauma from two decades of professional football. But when Omalu's examination revealed extensive chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE), a degenerative brain disease linked to repetitive head impacts (Omalu et al., 2005), the National Football League's response was swift and uncompromising: demand retraction, dismiss the methodology, and apply institutional pressure to silence inconvenient research.

This reaction revealed a coordination crisis that would test whether ordinary people could challenge one of America's most powerful entertainment institutions. The NFL's approach represented a familiar pattern: when systematic problems threaten institutional interests, those with formal authority often pursue individual organizational goals that exclude other stakeholders from meaningful participation. The league's Mild Traumatic Brain Injury committee, led by team physicians, focused on protecting organizational liability and maintaining revenue streams rather than establishing truth about brain injury or preventing future harm (Fainaru & Fainaru-Wada, 2013).

For nearly a decade, this monopolistic approach appeared successful. The NFL possessed the resources to fund research, the platform to shape public discourse, and the institutional authority to set player safety policies. Independent researchers faced funding difficulties, affected families lacked medical credibility, and journalists encountered access restrictions. The system seemed designed to ensure that those with the most authentic concern about player welfare—the players and families experiencing brain injury consequences—had the least influence over policies meant to protect them.

Yet by 2016, the same institution that had categorically denied any connection between football and brain disease was implementing comprehensive concussion protocols, funding independent research, and publicly acknowledging the relationship between repetitive head impacts and long-term neurological damage. This transformation didn't occur through regulatory intervention, legal mandate, or corporate social responsibility initiatives. Instead, it emerged from a coordination network that included brain injury researchers, affected families, investigative journalists, and legal advocates who discovered they could advance the same shared objective—establishing truth about CTE and preventing future harm—through their distinctive but equivalent contributions.

Beyond Expert Authority: The Democratic Promise of Coordination Networks

What makes this transformation philosophically significant extends far beyond sports policy. The NFL case demonstrates that effective ethical action can emerge when diverse stakeholders unite around genuine network goals—collective objectives that different approaches can advance equivalently while preserving stakeholder authenticity. This challenges dominant assumptions about how systematic ethical problems get addressed and who has the authority to participate meaningfully in solutions.

Traditional approaches to multi-stakeholder coordination typically require either content agreement (everyone must share beliefs and values), procedural compromise (everyone must moderate their positions), hierarchical authority (experts or officials make decisions for others), or market mechanisms (economic incentives align individual interests). Each approach has significant limitations: content agreement eliminates diversity, compromise reduces effectiveness, hierarchy excludes stakeholders, and market mechanisms treat ethical coordination as economic transaction.

The coordination network that transformed NFL policy succeeded through a different mechanism: capability equivalence around shared network goals. Families contributed experiential knowledge and moral authority through testimony about player decline; scientists contributed research methodology and peer-reviewed evidence through brain banking and analysis; lawyers contributed procedural accountability through litigation and discovery; journalists contributed transparency and public education through investigative reporting. These different approaches enhanced rather than competed with each other because they advanced the same collective objective through functionally equivalent but distinctive contributions.

This pattern reveals possibilities for democratizing ethical authority by showing that coordination competence—rather than formal credentials, institutional position, or specialized expertise—can create legitimate influence over systematic problems. NFL families gained authority not through medical training but through demonstrated capability for advancing brain injury research and prevention goals that others came to value and rely upon. Their questions became compelling, their engagement became essential, and their voices became influential because they consistently contributed to shared objectives that transcended individual purposes.

The democratic promise lies in recognizing that anyone confronting systematic ethical problems can potentially develop similar coordination competence through authentic engagement with shared objectives. This doesn't require abandoning professional expertise or institutional authority, but rather recognizing that effective coordination often emerges when diverse types of knowledge and capability contribute equivalently to collective goals that no single approach can achieve alone.

Types of Network Contributions: Making Coordination Accessible

The NFL transformation succeeded because it recognized that effective coordination requires diverse types of contributions rather than uniform expertise or leadership capability. This insight makes ethical action accessible by showing multiple ways people can participate meaningfully in addressing systematic problems:

  • Knowledge Contributors provide expertise, research, local experience, or specialized understanding that advances shared objectives. NFL families contributed experiential knowledge of brain injury symptoms; scientists contributed research methodologies; medical professionals contributed clinical expertise. Each type of knowledge enhanced the others when focused on the same network goal.
  • Connection Contributors link different stakeholder groups, translate between communities, and facilitate communication across cultural or professional differences. Investigative journalists connected families with researchers; legal advocates connected individual cases with policy implications; medical professionals connected research findings with clinical practice.
  • Resource Contributors offer funding, platform access, institutional support, or material resources that enable coordination activities. Legal firms provided litigation resources; universities provided research infrastructure; media organizations provided platforms for public education; advocacy organizations provided coordination support.
  • Amplification Contributors handle communication, education, advocacy, or public engagement that extends coordination reach and impact. Journalists amplified research findings and family stories; advocacy organizations amplified policy implications; affected families amplified prevention messages to other families and communities.
  • Implementation Contributors focus on practical execution, monitoring, maintenance, or direct service that translates coordination into concrete outcomes. Medical professionals implemented new protocols; league officials adapted policies; coaches modified training practices; equipment manufacturers developed safety technologies.
  • Support Contributors provide sustained attention, documentation, encouragement, or logistical assistance that maintains coordination over time. Family members maintained research participation across years; advocates provided emotional support during difficult periods; researchers documented coordination processes for learning and replication.

This framework demonstrates that coordination networks need distributed contributions rather than centralized leadership, making effective ethical action accessible to anyone willing to develop coordination capabilities around genuinely shared objectives. Whether someone leads, follows, supports, connects, amplifies, or implements, all contributions can be essential for coordination success.

Theoretical Contribution: Network Goals Enable Coordinated Authenticity

The NFL case illustrates a broader theoretical insight that addresses a fundamental challenge in applied ethics: how can diverse stakeholders coordinate effectively around systematic problems without requiring them to abandon their authentic values, approaches, or identities? Traditional coordination theories struggle with this challenge because they assume coordination requires convergence—shared beliefs, common methods, or unified frameworks.

Network-goal coordination offers an alternative by focusing on shared objectives that different approaches can advance equivalently rather than requiring methodological or values convergence. When stakeholders unite around genuine network goals, their different approaches become mutually reinforcing rather than competing because they contribute to the same collective outcome through functionally equivalent but distinctive methods.

This creates what might be called "coordinated authenticity"—the ability to work together effectively while remaining true to diverse values, methods, and cultural commitments, specifically in service of collective goals rather than individual purposes. NFL families maintained their focus on memorial and advocacy; scientists maintained their commitment to peer review and evidence; lawyers maintained their procedural approach to accountability; journalists maintained their investigative methodology. Yet all approaches contributed equivalently to establishing CTE truth and preventing future harm.

This framework addresses several limitations in existing coordination theory. Social movement theory explains grassroots mobilization but struggles with professional integration as equivalent contributors rather than targets or allies (McAdam et al., 1996). Network governance theory explains distributed decision-making but typically coordinates across different objectives rather than focusing diverse approaches on the same shared goal (Ostrom, 2010). Public policy theory explains stakeholder engagement but often treats it as input to expert decision-making rather than equivalent contribution to collective outcomes.

Network-goal coordination explains coordination persistence and cultural transferability that these alternatives cannot fully capture. When stakeholders pursue genuine shared objectives rather than temporarily aligned individual interests, coordination persists through changing circumstances because the collective goal remains meaningful even when individual conditions change. When coordination focuses on functional capabilities rather than specific cultural content, coordination approaches transfer across different contexts while adapting to local conditions and values.

Preview of Evidence: Universal Patterns Across Contexts

The NFL transformation represents one instance of coordination patterns that appear across dramatically different contexts, time periods, and cultural settings. Dutch communities have coordinated flood management for over 800 years by enabling farmers, engineers, merchants, and political representatives to contribute equivalently to regional flood protection through their distinctive capabilities. Platform cooperatives like Stocksy demonstrate how workers, developers, and users can coordinate around democratic digital rights while maintaining economic viability and technological innovation (Scholz, 2016). Wangari Maathai's Green Belt Movement shows how village women, scientists, international organizations, and government agencies can coordinate environmental restoration from local tree-planting to international policy influence (Berkes, 2007).

These cases share common coordination principles despite their differences in historical period, cultural context, problem domain, and stakeholder composition. All involve diverse stakeholders uniting around genuine network goals that their different approaches can advance equivalently. All demonstrate bootstrap authority development—normative legitimacy emerging through coordination competence rather than formal credentials. All show capability equivalence enabling different approaches to contribute equally to shared objectives without requiring methodological convergence or values abandonment.

This pattern suggests that network-goal coordination represents a genuine phenomenon with universal applicability for addressing systematic coordination challenges, rather than a context-specific solution or temporary alignment of interests. The framework builds on insights from coordination theory and commons management (Ostrom, 2010) while focusing on how diverse stakeholders can unite around shared objectives that preserve their authentic approaches rather than requiring convergence (Mertzman, in preparation). This creates possibilities for addressing systematic ethical problems through coordination competence that anyone can develop rather than requiring specialized expertise or formal authority.

The following analysis demonstrates how these coordination principles operate across diverse contexts while providing practical guidance for building coordination networks around systematic ethical problems that require collective action but respect individual authenticity and cultural difference.

II. Pattern Recognition Across Contexts: Network Goals Enable Coordination

Successful coordination networks share a common structure: diverse stakeholders unite around genuine network goals that their different approaches can advance equivalently, while developing bootstrap authority through demonstrated coordination competence. This pattern appears across dramatically different contexts, time periods, and cultural settings, revealing universal principles that apply whether you're dealing with medieval flood control, contemporary brain injury research, digital labor organizing, or environmental restoration.

The NFL Transformation: From Monopolistic Control to Network Coordination

The NFL case illustrates both coordination failure and transformation success, providing a clear contrast between individual institutional goals and genuine network goals.

Initial Coordination Failure (2005-2010)

The NFL's initial approach failed precisely because it pursued individual institutional goals that other stakeholders couldn't advance equivalently. The league's Mild Traumatic Brain Injury committee, chaired by Dr. Elliot Pellman, focused on protecting league liability, maintaining revenue models, and preserving competitive entertainment value. These objectives created systematic exclusion:

  • Families couldn't advance league protection - their concerns about player welfare directly conflicted with liability minimization
  • Scientists couldn't maintain NFL revenue models - research revealing brain injury risks threatened the entertainment product
  • Legal professionals couldn't preserve competitive practices - accountability mechanisms conflicted with league control
  • Journalists couldn't protect institutional interests - investigation threatened corporate image management

When Bennet Omalu published CTE findings, the NFL's response followed individual institutional goal logic: demand retraction, question methodology, apply pressure. This revealed the fundamental limitation—the committee organized around institutional protection rather than truth establishment (Fainaru & Fainaru-Wada, 2013).

Coordination Transformation (2010-2020)

Transformation occurred when stakeholders recognized a genuine network goal: "establishing CTE truth and preventing future harm." This objective differed fundamentally because each group could advance it through their authentic approaches without compromising their core purposes:

  • Families' Authentic Contribution: Experiential documentation and moral witness. Families like those of Dave Duerson, who preserved brain tissue for research and provided detailed behavioral documentation, contributed irreplaceable evidence while honoring their loved ones' memory. Their testimony connected individual stories to larger patterns, creating moral authority that research alone couldn't achieve.
  • Scientists' Authentic Contribution: Research methodology and peer-reviewed evidence. Dr. Ann McKee's brain banking efforts at Boston University gained legitimacy through producing reliable research that other stakeholders could use (McKee et al., 2013). Scientific authority emerged through functional contribution to the shared network goal rather than academic credentials alone.
  • Legal Professionals' Authentic Contribution: Procedural accountability and information discovery. The class action lawsuit succeeded not through adversarial victory but through information revelation that enabled more effective coordination. Legal discovery provided evidence that scientists used for research direction and families used for advocacy strategy.
  • Journalists' Authentic Contribution: Public education and transparency. Investigative reporting by Mark Fainaru-Wada and Steve Fainaru created platforms enabling different stakeholders to contribute equivalently to public understanding and prevention, revealing information that enhanced all other approaches (Fainaru & Fainaru-Wada, 2013).

Key Success Factors

  • Enhancement Rather Than Competition: Different approaches genuinely enhanced each other's effectiveness. Family testimony improved scientific research quality; scientific findings strengthened legal accountability cases; media coverage amplified both while providing new information that improved all approaches.
  • Sustained Focus on Collective Objective: Coordination persisted through changing media attention, political priorities, and economic conditions because stakeholders remained committed to the shared network goal while adapting their methods.
  • Bootstrap Authority Development: Authority emerged through demonstrated coordination competence. Families gained influence not through formal training but through sustained, authentic engagement that others came to value and rely upon for advancing shared objectives.

Dutch Water Boards: 800 Years of Coordination Resilience

The Dutch Waterschappen provide unparalleled evidence of network-goal coordination's temporal resilience and cultural transferability. Since the 12th century, these democratic institutions have coordinated flood management across diverse stakeholder groups while adapting to environmental changes, political transformations, and technological revolutions.

Historical Development and Network Goal Evolution

  • Medieval Origins (1200s-1400s): Local communities facing catastrophic flooding recognized that effective flood protection required coordination across different types of knowledge and capability. The network goal of "regional flood protection" emerged because no single group possessed all necessary capabilities:
    • Local farmers had intimate knowledge of seasonal water patterns, soil drainage characteristics, and land use impacts but lacked technical engineering expertise
    • Technical craftsmen possessed construction and hydraulic knowledge but needed local environmental knowledge and political coordination
    • Merchants and landowners had economic resources and political connections but required both technical and local knowledge for effective flood prevention
    • Political authorities could coordinate across jurisdictions but needed technical, local, and economic input for effective decision-making
  • Renaissance Expansion (1400s-1600s): As Dutch commerce expanded, the network goal evolved to include economic development alongside flood protection. Different stakeholders contributed equivalently to "flood protection enabling economic prosperity":
    • Engineers developed increasingly sophisticated drainage and pumping technologies, contributing technical innovation
    • Farmers adapted agricultural practices to support drainage infrastructure while maintaining productivity
    • Merchants provided economic analysis showing how flood protection enabled trade route development and commercial growth
    • Political representatives coordinated regional policies that balanced local autonomy with inter-regional coordination needs
  • Modern Adaptation (1800s-present): The network goal has continuously evolved while maintaining its essential character. Contemporary water boards coordinate around "sustainable water management and climate adaptation," enabling diverse contributions:
    • Environmental scientists contribute climate modeling and ecological assessment capabilities
    • Community representatives contribute local knowledge and democratic participation in planning processes
    • Engineering professionals contribute technical design and infrastructure management expertise
    • Economic analysts contribute cost-benefit analysis and sustainable financing approaches

Institutional Resilience Mechanisms

  • Bootstrap Authority Preservation: Across eight centuries, water board authority has consistently derived from demonstrated coordination competence rather than external appointment. Board members gain legitimacy through effective flood management outcomes that communities experience directly. This creates authority that persists through political changes because it's grounded in functional competence rather than formal credentials.
  • Adaptive Coordination Methods: The same network goal has been pursued through evolving coordination methods. Medieval assemblies, Renaissance guild participation, Enlightenment representative democracy, and contemporary participatory planning all achieved equivalent coordination outcomes through culturally appropriate but functionally similar processes.
  • Capability Equivalence Maintenance: Different types of knowledge have consistently contributed equivalently to flood protection across technological and social changes. Local environmental knowledge, technical engineering expertise, economic analysis, and political coordination have remained functionally equivalent contributions even as their specific content evolved dramatically.
  • Network Goal Authentication: The persistence of genuine network goals explains the system's resilience. "Flood protection" has remained meaningful to all stakeholder types across centuries because each can authentically advance it through their distinctive approaches without compromising their core purposes or identities.

Platform Cooperatives: Democratic Digital Alternatives

Worker-owned digital platforms demonstrate network-goal coordination's applicability to contemporary technological coordination challenges. Cases like Stocksy (photography marketplace), Up & Go (cleaning services platform), and Fairmondo (e-commerce alternative) show how diverse stakeholders can unite around "democratic digital rights and equitable ownership" while challenging corporate platform monopolies.

Coordination Challenge and Network Goal Identification

  • The Platform Economy Problem: Traditional platforms like Uber, Amazon, and Facebook concentrate ownership and decision-making power while extracting value from worker and user contributions. This creates systematic exclusion where those creating platform value lack voice in platform governance.
  • Network Goal Recognition: Platform cooperatives succeeded by recognizing that different stakeholders shared an authentic interest in "democratic digital rights and equitable ownership"—a network goal that enables diverse contributions without requiring stakeholders to abandon their authentic purposes:
    • Workers want fair compensation, workplace autonomy, and voice in decisions affecting their labor
    • Users want platform reliability, fair pricing, and protection from exploitative practices
    • Developers want technical innovation opportunities and sustainable development practices
    • Community advocates want democratic alternatives to corporate technological control

Implementation Through Capability Equivalence

  • Stocksy Photography Cooperative: Founded in 2013, Stocksy demonstrates how diverse stakeholders contribute equivalently to democratic platform governance:
    • Worker-Owner Contributions: Professional photographers contribute content creation, quality curation, and market knowledge through their artistic and commercial expertise. They participate in governance decisions about platform direction, pricing policies, and profit distribution while maintaining creative autonomy.
    • Technical Developer Contributions: Software developers contribute platform architecture, user interface design, and security systems through cooperative development practices. They balance technical innovation with democratic accountability, creating platforms that serve user needs rather than maximizing data extraction.
    • User Community Contributions: Platform users contribute market validation, feedback on functionality, and promotion of cooperative alternatives through their engagement choices. Users become active participants in platform development rather than passive consumers of corporate products.
    • Cooperative Board Contributions: Elected representatives contribute strategic planning, financial oversight, and stakeholder coordination through democratic governance processes. Board authority derives from member election and demonstrated coordination competence rather than external investment or corporate hierarchy.

Economic Viability and Network Goal Advancement

  • Financial Performance: Platform cooperatives prove that democratic governance enhances rather than threatens economic viability. Stocksy has maintained profitability while distributing ownership broadly, demonstrating that network-goal coordination can challenge corporate monopolies through sustained coordination rather than requiring stakeholders to abandon market participation.
  • Innovation Through Cooperation: Cooperative platforms generate innovations that corporate alternatives cannot achieve:
    • Transparent algorithmic decision-making that users understand and can influence
    • Profit-sharing models that align platform success with contributor welfare
    • Democratic technology development that prioritizes user needs over advertising revenue
    • Cooperative education and skill development that strengthens the entire network rather than just extracting individual labor
  • Cultural Transferability: Similar cooperative platforms have emerged across different cultural contexts—European cooperative platforms, Latin American solidarity economy networks, and African cooperative technology initiatives—all organizing around equivalent network goals while adapting to local cultural and economic conditions.

Green Belt Movement: Environmental Coordination Across Scales

Wangari Maathai's Green Belt Movement demonstrates how network-goal coordination can scale from village-level organizing to international policy influence while maintaining grassroots democratic participation and environmental effectiveness.

Multi-Scale Network Goal Development

  • Village-Level Origins (1970s): The movement began when rural Kenyan women identified connections between deforestation, soil erosion, water scarcity, and family welfare. The initial network goal of "environmental restoration and community empowerment" enabled diverse contributions:
    • Women's Community Contributions: Rural women contributed daily environmental observation, traditional ecological knowledge, and community organizing capabilities through tree-planting activities, nursery management, and village coordination. Their environmental work connected directly to family welfare and community survival.
    • Local Technical Contributions: Agricultural extension workers and forestry professionals contributed scientific knowledge about appropriate tree species, planting techniques, and ecosystem restoration through technical support that enhanced community organizing efforts.
    • Educational Institution Contributions: Universities and research organizations contributed ecological research, monitoring methodologies, and documentation through academic partnerships that validated community environmental knowledge while providing scientific frameworks.

National and International Scaling (1980s-2000s)

  • Policy Integration: As the movement demonstrated environmental restoration effectiveness, the network goal evolved to include policy influence. Different stakeholders contributed equivalently to "environmental sustainability and democratic participation":
    • Grassroots Organization Contributions: Women's groups contributed proof-of-concept environmental restoration, community organization models, and democratic participation practices through sustained tree-planting networks that demonstrated alternative development approaches.
    • NGO and International Contributions: Development organizations contributed funding, technical support, and international policy access through partnerships that amplified grassroots efforts while maintaining community control over local environmental work.
    • Government Agency Contributions: Eventually, government forestry and environmental agencies contributed regulatory support, program integration, and policy adoption through formal recognition of community environmental management capabilities.
    • Academic and Research Contributions: International environmental research networks contributed scientific validation, global environmental framework integration, and policy analysis through scholarly work that demonstrated the movement's broader environmental significance.

Bootstrap Authority and Democratic Environmental Leadership

  • Authority Through Environmental Competence: Wangari Maathai and rural women gained international environmental authority not through formal environmental credentials but through demonstrated environmental restoration competence that produced measurable ecological and social outcomes.
  • Democratic Leadership Development: The movement created opportunities for ordinary women to develop coordination capabilities and environmental leadership through authentic engagement with shared environmental objectives. This democratized environmental authority by showing it emerges through coordination competence rather than technical training.
  • Persistence Through Political Resistance: The movement maintained effectiveness through political repression, leadership harassment, and government opposition because the network goal remained meaningful to all stakeholders, while coordination competence created authority that persisted independently of formal political support.
  • Cultural and Environmental Integration: The movement succeeded by connecting environmental restoration to cultural values, economic needs, and political aspirations in ways that enhanced rather than threatened community identity and autonomy.

Cross-Context Pattern Recognition

These four cases—spanning medieval flood management, contemporary brain injury research, digital platform development, and environmental restoration—reveal universal coordination principles that apply across dramatically different contexts:

Universal Network Goal Characteristics

  • Authentic Stakeholder Advancement: Genuine network goals enable all stakeholder types to advance collective objectives through their authentic approaches without requiring methodological convergence, values abandonment, or identity compromise. Dutch flood protection, CTE truth and prevention, democratic digital rights, and environmental sustainability all possess this characteristic.
  • Enhancement Rather Than Competition: Different approaches genuinely enhance each other's effectiveness when focused on authentic network goals. Technical knowledge enhances local knowledge; community organizing enhances scientific research; democratic governance enhances economic viability; environmental restoration enhances community empowerment.
  • Temporal Persistence: Network goals that are genuinely shared persist across changing external circumstances because they remain meaningful to all stakeholder types even when individual conditions change. Flood protection remained important through political transformations; brain injury prevention persisted through media cycles; democratic technology remained valuable despite corporate opposition; environmental restoration continued through political repression.

Universal Capability Equivalence Patterns

  • Functional Equivalence Across Difference: Different approaches achieve equivalent coordination outcomes through distinctive methods when focused on shared network goals. Local environmental knowledge ≡ scientific ecological research ≡ policy advocacy ≡ international development support when all advance environmental sustainability through their authentic capabilities.
  • Knowledge Integration Without Homogenization: Diverse types of knowledge contribute equivalently to coordination effectiveness without requiring convergence. Technical engineering expertise + local environmental knowledge + economic analysis + political coordination = effective flood management, where each contribution type is functionally equivalent but maintains its distinctiveness.
  • Cultural Transferability Through Capability Focus: Coordination approaches transfer across cultural contexts because they focus on functional capabilities rather than specific cultural content. Democratic participation, technical competence, local knowledge, and resource coordination appear across all successful cases while being expressed through culturally appropriate but functionally equivalent methods.

Universal Bootstrap Authority Development

  • Competence-Based Legitimacy: In all cases, authority emerged through demonstrated coordination competence rather than formal credentials, institutional position, or external validation. Dutch farmers, NFL families, platform cooperative members, and environmental organizers all gained influence through sustained coordination effectiveness that others came to value and rely upon.
  • Democratic Accessibility: Coordination competence can be developed by anyone willing to engage authentically with shared network objectives through sustained attention and contribution. This makes effective coordination accessible to ordinary people confronting systematic problems, regardless of specialized training or institutional position.
  • Authority Persistence: Bootstrap authority persists across changing circumstances because it's grounded in functional competence for advancing shared objectives rather than external institutional support. This creates coordination resilience that survives political transitions, leadership changes, and external pressure.

These patterns suggest that network-goal coordination represents a genuine phenomenon with universal applicability for addressing systematic coordination challenges across diverse contexts, cultures, and time periods.

III. Implementation Framework: Building Coordination Networks

The coordination patterns observed across successful networks translate into practical tools for anyone confronting systematic ethical problems and seeking to build effective response networks. These tools have been tested across contexts from medieval flood management to contemporary AI ethics, providing concrete guidance for developing coordination competence and building effective networks.

Initial Assessment: Can You Contribute to Addressing This Issue?

Before attempting coordination, conduct a systematic assessment of whether you can contribute authentically to addressing the problem. This assessment prevents wasted effort and ensures your engagement serves genuine coordination rather than individual expression.

Authentic Connection Assessment

  • Personal Connection Evaluation:
    • Direct Experience: Do you or people close to you experience this problem directly? NFL families had immediate experience with CTE symptoms; Green Belt women experienced daily environmental degradation; platform workers faced algorithmic management.
    • Professional Responsibility: Does your work role create responsibility for addressing this issue? Water board engineers had professional flood management duties; research scientists had investigative responsibilities; legal professionals had accountability functions.
    • Community Impact: Does this problem affect communities you're part of or care about? Environmental restoration affected entire Kenyan villages; flood management protected Dutch regional communities; workplace democracy affected broader worker communities.
    • Moral Concern: Do you have authentic ethical concern that connects to your values? This must be genuine rather than performative—sustainable coordination requires authentic motivation.
  • Temporal Integration Capability:
    • Past Understanding: Can you articulate how historical patterns contribute to current problems? Successful coordinators integrate past experience (historical flooding, brain injury patterns, environmental degradation) with present coordination efforts.
    • Present Engagement: Can you connect current symptoms to underlying coordination failures? Effective coordinators see systematic patterns rather than isolated incidents.
    • Future Prevention: Can you envision how coordination might prevent future harm while respecting different approaches? This requires seeing beyond individual solutions to coordination possibilities.
  • Sustained Engagement Preparation:
    • Motivation Source: What authentic values will sustain your engagement when coordination becomes difficult? Dutch farmers maintained flood management through centuries; environmental organizers persisted through political repression; worker cooperatives sustained democracy through economic pressures.
    • Realistic Expectations: Are you prepared for slow progress, setbacks, and adaptation rather than quick victories? Effective coordination develops over years rather than months.
    • Learning Orientation: Are you willing to modify your approach based on what you learn from others rather than demanding others adopt your methods?

Contribution Capability Assessment

  • Knowledge and Skill Inventory:
    • Specialized Knowledge: What do you know that others might find valuable? This could be technical expertise, local knowledge, historical experience, or cultural understanding.
    • Communication Abilities: Can you translate between different stakeholder groups or explain complex issues in accessible ways?
    • Organizational Skills: Do you have capabilities in planning, coordination, documentation, or project management that could serve network goals?
    • Resource Access: Do you have connections, funding, platform access, or institutional position that could support coordination efforts?
  • Contribution Style Assessment:
    • Leadership Readiness: Are you prepared to take initiative, make decisions, and represent coordination efforts publicly when needed?
    • Collaborative Support: Are you willing to follow others' leadership when they have better capabilities for specific coordination tasks?
    • Behind-the-Scenes Work: Can you contribute effectively through documentation, resource provision, or infrastructure support without public recognition?
    • Connection Building: Do you have abilities or opportunities to link different stakeholder groups that might not otherwise coordinate?

Network Goal Potential Assessment

  • Coordination Necessity: Does addressing this issue require capabilities that exceed what any single group possesses? Problems requiring coordination typically need diverse knowledge, resources, and implementation capabilities.
  • Enhancement Opportunity: Might different approaches enhance rather than compete with each other? Look for situations where scientific research could enhance community knowledge, legal accountability could strengthen advocacy efforts, or technical solutions could support grassroots organizing.
  • Authentic Goal Identification: Could stakeholders achieve their genuine goals through coordination rather than requiring others to abandon their values? Genuine network goals preserve stakeholder authenticity while enabling collective action.

Advanced Stakeholder Mapping: Finding Your Coordination Network

Effective coordination requires mapping potential partners systematically across different types of legitimate concern and contribution capability. This process identifies who might contribute to shared objectives while respecting their authentic motivations and approaches.

Knowledge Contributors: Evidence and Expertise

  • Professional Researchers and Analysts:
    • Academic Researchers: University scientists, policy analysts, and social researchers who might find intellectual value in understanding your issue. Example: Brain injury researchers gained professional value from studying CTE while contributing to family advocacy goals.
    • Investigative Journalists: Media professionals whose investigation responsibilities align with transparency and accountability around your issue. Example: Sports journalists investigating NFL policies found professional purpose in CTE truth-seeking.
    • Technical Specialists: Engineers, medical professionals, legal experts, or other specialists whose expertise relates to your coordination challenge. Example: Hydraulic engineers contributed technical knowledge to flood management while advancing professional flood prevention responsibilities.
  • Experiential Knowledge Holders:
    • Directly Affected Communities: People experiencing the problem who possess lived experience and local knowledge. Example: Platform workers understand algorithmic management effects that technical designers might miss.
    • Community Observers: People whose daily life provides relevant observation and cultural knowledge. Example: Rural women observed environmental degradation patterns that scientific monitoring might not capture.
    • Historical Experience Holders: People with long-term experience of similar problems or attempted solutions. Example: Dutch farmers possessed generations of flood management knowledge that enhanced technical engineering approaches.

Connection Contributors: Bridge-Building and Translation

  • Cross-Community Connectors:
    • Multi-Stakeholder Organizations: Groups that regularly work across the boundaries relevant to your issue and have relationships in different communities.
    • Cultural Translators: People who understand different stakeholder communities well enough to facilitate communication and build understanding across cultural or professional differences.
    • Convening Capabilities: Organizations or individuals with space, resources, and credibility to bring different groups together for coordination discussions.
  • Platform and Communication Access:
    • Media Access: People with platforms, audiences, or communication channels that could amplify coordination efforts appropriately.
    • Educational Institutions: Schools, universities, or training organizations that could provide learning and development support for coordination capabilities.
    • Digital Platform Access: Technical capabilities for online coordination, documentation, or communication across distributed networks.

Resource Contributors: Implementation and Support

  • Material Resource Access:
    • Funding Sources: Organizations or individuals with financial resources that could support coordination activities, research, or implementation efforts.
    • Infrastructure Access: Space, equipment, technology, or other material resources that coordination efforts might require.
    • Institutional Support: Organizations whose mission or responsibility could provide structural support for coordination activities.
  • Implementation Capabilities:
    • Service Delivery: Organizations or individuals capable of implementing solutions or providing direct services to affected communities.
    • Policy Implementation: People with authority or capability to implement policy changes or regulatory responses.
    • Monitoring and Evaluation: Capabilities for tracking coordination effectiveness and learning from successes and failures.

Existing Authority Holders: Working With, Around, or Against Power

  • Formal Decision-Making Authority:
    • Regulatory Agencies: Government bodies with legal authority over aspects of your issue who might support, ignore, or oppose coordination efforts.
    • Corporate Leadership: Business executives or institutional leaders whose decisions affect your issue and coordination possibilities.
    • Elected Representatives: Political leaders whose policy authority could support or hinder coordination efforts.
  • Institutional Power Centers:
    • Professional Associations: Organizations representing people with authority in relevant professional domains.
    • Labor Organizations: Unions or worker representatives with collective bargaining or workplace authority.
    • Community Leadership: Formal and informal leaders within affected communities who have influence over community participation in coordination.
  • Strategic Relationship Assessment:
    • Potential Allies: Authority holders whose authentic interests might align with coordination goals and who could provide institutional support.
    • Neutral Parties: Authority holders who might not actively support coordination but could be approached for specific assistance or neutrality.
    • Opposition Forces: Authority holders whose interests conflict with coordination goals and who might actively resist coordination efforts.

Developing Bootstrap Authority: Systematic Competence Building

The most powerful insight from successful networks is that normative authority—the legitimacy to influence outcomes—emerges through demonstrated coordination competence rather than credentials or position. This authority can be developed systematically through focused capability building.

Evidence Assessment Capability Development

  • Information Quality Evaluation:
    • Source Credibility Assessment: Learn to evaluate the reliability and bias of different information sources relevant to your issue. Develop capability to distinguish peer-reviewed research, investigative journalism, experiential testimony, and advocacy material while recognizing the appropriate role each plays in coordination.
    • Multiple Perspective Integration: Actively seek out different viewpoints on your issue, especially from stakeholder groups whose approaches differ from yours. IV. Limitations and Appropriate Applications

Network-goal coordination faces significant limitations that require honest acknowledgment alongside its applications. Understanding when the framework fails, causes harm, or proves inappropriate is essential for responsible implementation and prevents misapplication that could damage the communities and causes it aims to serve.

When Genuine Network Goals Cannot Be Identified

  • Zero-Sum Resource Conflicts: When stakeholder success genuinely requires others' failure due to limited resources that cannot be expanded through coordination, network-goal identification may be structurally impossible. Urban development versus habitat preservation often creates genuine conflicts where physical space limitations prevent coordination solutions. In downtown Portland, efforts to coordinate affordable housing development with environmental protection failed because preserving critical wetlands genuinely conflicted with housing construction—different approaches couldn't enhance each other when they required mutually exclusive land use decisions.
  • Irreconcilable Value Conflicts: When stakeholder values create fundamental conflicts that prevent authentic shared objective identification without requiring stakeholders to compromise their core commitments. Conflicts over reproductive rights frequently involve irreconcilable disagreements about personhood, bodily autonomy, and religious freedom that prevent network-goal identification preserving stakeholder authenticity. Attempts to coordinate "pregnancy support" often fail because pro-choice and pro-life advocates hold genuinely incompatible views about fundamental questions that network coordination cannot resolve.
  • Emergency Response Requirements: When immediate action is needed to prevent harm and relationship building for network-goal identification would create unacceptable delays. Public health emergencies like COVID-19 often required rapid expert-led responses where coordination development would have cost lives. Natural disasters demanding immediate resource deployment, security threats requiring rapid defensive action, and medical emergencies requiring immediate intervention all prioritize speed over stakeholder coordination.

Detailed Failure Analysis: When Network-Goal Coordination Goes Wrong

  • Environmental Justice Coalition Failure: A well-intentioned coalition attempted to unite around "environmental health for all" in an industrial community experiencing multiple pollution sources, but coordination failed despite significant resources and stakeholder commitment. The apparent network goal was actually a pseudo-network goal because different stakeholders meant fundamentally different things by "environmental health":
    • Environmental groups wanted minimal pollution exposure achieved through industrial restrictions and regulatory enforcement
    • Community organizations wanted economic opportunities and improved housing that required some industrial activity for employment
    • Labor unions wanted workplace safety and employment security that conflicted with plant closures environmental groups sought
    • Local businesses wanted economically sustainable environmental improvement that conflicted with costly regulatory compliance
    These approaches conflicted rather than enhanced each other because stakeholders faced genuine tradeoffs between environmental protection and economic survival. Achieving apparent coordination required stakeholders to compromise their authentic purposes—environmental groups accepting pollution levels they considered unsafe, communities accepting economic limitations they considered unjust, unions accepting job losses for environmental benefits.
  • Corporate Stakeholder Engagement Manipulation: A major extractive industry corporation used network-goal language to co-opt environmental and community opposition while continuing harmful practices. The company claimed shared commitment to "sustainable development" with environmental groups and affected communities, but structured coordination to serve corporate interests:
    • Pseudo-Network Goal Creation: "Sustainable development" meant different things to different stakeholders but company-controlled processes defined it to preserve extractive operations while requiring environmental groups to moderate demands
    • Authority Manipulation: Community representatives were included in advisory processes that provided legitimacy appearance but lacked decision-making authority over substantive corporate practices
    • Resource Dependency: Environmental groups became financially dependent on corporate partnership funding that constrained criticism and independent organizing
    This manipulation delayed environmental protection while creating appearance of stakeholder cooperation, demonstrating how network-goal language can mask rather than resolve power imbalances.

Potential Misuse Scenarios: How the Framework Could Cause Harm

  • Power Imbalance Masking: Network-goal coordination could be misused to legitimize dominant stakeholder interests while requiring subordinate stakeholders to provide credibility for harmful practices. Corporate "stakeholder engagement" around "sustainable development" often defines sustainability to preserve corporate control while claiming shared commitment with environmental groups and communities. The corporation benefits from legitimacy while environmental groups provide credibility for practices they would otherwise oppose, wasting resources on ineffective coordination while creating appearance of progress.
  • Co-optation of Resistance Movements: The framework could be misused to co-opt resistance movements by reframing their demands as "shared network goals" that require moderation, compromise, or patience while maintaining harmful systems. Claiming that police reform movements and police departments share "community safety" as a network goal ignores fundamental disagreements about what safety means, how to achieve it, and whether existing institutions can be reformed rather than replaced. Resistance movements may be diverted from necessary system change into coordination efforts that preserve existing power structures while creating appearance of reform progress.
  • Delay Tactics for Urgent Problems: Complex coordination development could be used to delay necessary immediate action by claiming that "network-goal coordination" is needed before action can be taken on urgent problems. Using coordination requirements to delay environmental protection while ecosystems suffer irreversible damage, or delaying emergency response while coordination develops around crisis situations requiring immediate intervention, prioritizes process over outcomes in ways that cause harm.
  • Cultural Appropriation and Imposition: Network-goal coordination could be inappropriately applied to cultural contexts where it conflicts with traditional decision-making processes, community autonomy, or cultural values about authority and participation. Imposing network-goal coordination approaches on Indigenous communities whose traditional governance systems already provide effective coordination through different cultural mechanisms would disrupt effective systems while appropriating coordination language and undermining community self-determination.

Cultural Boundaries and Respect

  • Indigenous Sovereignty and Self-Determination: Issues affecting Indigenous communities require recognition that outside coordination involvement may be culturally inappropriate regardless of good intentions. Indigenous governance systems often already provide effective coordination through traditional mechanisms that outside involvement would disrupt or undermine. Respect for sovereignty means recognizing when communities have effective systems that don't require external coordination "improvement" and when outside involvement constitutes cultural imposition rather than support.
  • Cultural Decision-Making Processes: When cultural contexts emphasize coordination approaches—consensus traditions, hierarchical authority structures, or traditional leadership systems—that make network-goal coordination inappropriate or harmful. Traditional communities where explicit coordination discussion conflicts with cultural decision-making processes, hierarchical cultures where distributed coordination threatens legitimate authority structures, and communities emphasizing consensus where network-goal language might disrupt established consensus-building practices all require cultural competency that respects existing effective systems.
  • Community Invitation and Autonomy: Network-goal coordination should only occur when affected communities genuinely invite outside involvement rather than having coordination imposed by well-intentioned outsiders. Community autonomy means recognizing when issues are appropriately addressed through internal community processes rather than multi-stakeholder coordination, and when outside involvement—however well-intentioned—would be intrusive rather than helpful.

When Alternative Approaches Are More Appropriate

  • Expert Authority for Technical Safety: Problems requiring specialized expertise that community engagement cannot safely replace necessitate professional authority and hierarchical coordination rather than stakeholder relationship building. Nuclear safety regulation, pharmaceutical development, aviation safety standards, and complex medical procedures all require technical expertise that exceeds community coordination capacity. Professional-community collaboration remains important for priority-setting and outcome assessment, but technical implementation requires expert authority.
  • Democratic Process for Value Conflicts: When stakeholder values are irreconcilable but decisions must be made through legitimate democratic authority rather than coordination. Electoral competition, constitutional law cases, and fundamental policy choices about resource allocation often require democratic deliberation and voting rather than coordination that might mask rather than resolve genuine disagreements about values and priorities.
  • Market Mechanisms for Resource Allocation: When individual choice and economic competition serve collective welfare more effectively than coordination. Market allocation of consumer goods, competitive innovation in technology development, and entrepreneurial business formation often achieve better outcomes through individual decision-making than through coordination processes that might stifle innovation or efficiency.
  • Emergency Response for Crisis Management: When immediate action is required and coordination development would delay necessary intervention. Emergency medical response, disaster relief, fire suppression, and security threats all require rapid expert-led action through established command structures rather than stakeholder coordination that would compromise response effectiveness.

Diagnostic Questions for Appropriate Application

Before applying network-goal coordination, practitioners should assess:

  • Genuine Shared Objective Possibility: Can all stakeholders advance a proposed network goal through their authentic approaches without compromising their core purposes? Do different approaches enhance rather than compete with each other? Would coordination persist through changing external circumstances?
  • Cultural Appropriateness: Is network-goal coordination appropriate for the cultural context, or would it conflict with traditional decision-making processes? Do affected communities invite coordination involvement, or would it be intrusive regardless of good intentions?
  • Urgency Assessment: Does the coordination timeline allow adequate relationship building without delaying necessary urgent action? Can emergency needs be addressed while longer-term coordination develops around prevention and improvement?
  • Power Dynamic Evaluation: Can all stakeholders participate authentically rather than being controlled by dominant interests? Are power imbalances managed appropriately to enable genuine participation rather than legitimacy provision for predetermined outcomes?

Recognition of these limitations is essential for ethical application. The framework provides tools for situations where network-goal coordination offers advantages over alternative approaches, but practitioners must recognize when other approaches—democratic process, expert authority, market mechanisms, emergency response—are more appropriate for particular problems and cultural contexts.

V. Implications for Applied Ethics: Democratic Coordination and Ethical Authority

Network-goal coordination contributes to applied ethics by demonstrating how objective normative outcomes can emerge from diverse stakeholder coordination through practical necessity rather than imposed uniformity or expert authority. This challenges fundamental assumptions about how ethical problems get addressed, who has authority to participate in solutions, and what constitutes legitimate ethical leadership in democratic societies.

Democratizing Ethical Authority: Beyond Expert Domination

Traditional approaches to multi-stakeholder coordination around ethical problems typically assume that effective action requires either content agreement (shared beliefs and values), procedural compromise (everyone moderates positions), hierarchical authority (experts or officials make decisions), or market mechanisms (economic incentives align individual interests). Each approach has significant limitations that network-goal coordination addresses:

  • Content Agreement Limitations: Requiring stakeholders to share beliefs and values eliminates the diversity that often makes coordination both necessary and effective. When environmental groups, labor unions, and community organizations must agree on fundamental values before addressing pollution problems, coordination becomes impossible precisely when diverse perspectives are most needed. The NFL transformation succeeded because families, scientists, lawyers, and journalists maintained their different values and approaches while advancing the same network goal.
  • Procedural Compromise Limitations: Requiring everyone to moderate their positions reduces the effectiveness that comes from stakeholders pursuing their authentic approaches with full commitment. When environmental advocates must accept pollution levels they consider unsafe, or when families must moderate their advocacy to accommodate institutional preferences, coordination produces lowest-common-denominator outcomes that serve no one well.
  • Hierarchical Authority Limitations: Excluding stakeholders from meaningful participation in decisions affecting them violates democratic principles while eliminating knowledge and capabilities essential for effective coordination. Expert-dominated approaches to brain injury research excluded the experiential knowledge that families possessed, making both the research and the policy responses less effective than they became through inclusive coordination.
  • Market Mechanism Limitations: Treating ethical coordination as economic transaction fails to address problems where market failures create the coordination challenges in the first place. Platform monopolies, environmental degradation, and workplace exploitation all represent market failures that market-based solutions cannot adequately address.

Network-goal coordination preserves stakeholder authenticity while enabling collective action by focusing on genuinely shared objectives that different approaches can advance equivalently. This creates what might be called "coordinated authenticity"—working together effectively while remaining true to diverse values and commitments, specifically in service of collective goals rather than individual purposes.

Theoretical Innovation: Capability-Based Rather Than Content-Based Coordination

The framework addresses a fundamental challenge in applied ethics: how can diverse stakeholders coordinate effectively around systematic problems without requiring them to abandon their authentic values, approaches, or cultural identities? This challenge has particular significance for democratic societies that value both diversity and collective action.

  • Beyond Consensus Models: Traditional deliberative democracy theory often assumes that effective coordination requires reasoned consensus around shared principles or procedures. Network-goal coordination shows that effective democratic action can emerge when stakeholders advance shared objectives through different but equivalent approaches, without requiring consensus on underlying values or methods. Dutch water management, platform cooperatives, and environmental movements all demonstrate sustained democratic coordination across fundamental disagreements about priorities, methods, and worldviews.
  • Beyond Interest Group Pluralism: Pluralist approaches to democratic coordination typically assume that diverse groups will pursue separate interests that political processes must balance or aggregate. Network-goal coordination shows how diverse groups can pursue genuinely shared objectives that their different approaches advance equivalently, creating coordination that transcends interest group competition. The NFL transformation succeeded not by balancing competing interests but by enabling different groups to advance the same objective through their distinctive capabilities.
  • Beyond Technocratic Governance: Expert-dominated approaches assume that complex problems require specialized knowledge that limits meaningful participation to trained professionals. Network-goal coordination demonstrates how technical expertise and experiential knowledge can contribute equivalently to collective outcomes when focused on shared objectives, democratizing participation without sacrificing effectiveness.

The theoretical innovation lies in focusing on functional capabilities rather than substantive content as the basis for coordination. When stakeholders unite around genuine network goals, their different approaches become mutually reinforcing rather than competing because they contribute to the same collective outcome through functionally equivalent but culturally and methodologically distinctive methods.

Bootstrap Authority and Democratic Legitimacy

The analysis demonstrates that ethical authority—the legitimacy to influence outcomes affecting systematic problems—can emerge through coordination competence rather than formal credentials, institutional position, or expert knowledge. This has profound implications for democratic theory and practice.

  • Authority Through Demonstrated Competence: NFL families, Dutch farmers, Kenyan women, and platform cooperative members all gained influence through sustained coordination effectiveness that others came to value and rely upon. Their authority derived not from formal training or institutional appointment but from demonstrated capability for advancing shared objectives that stakeholders recognized as valuable. This shows that ethical leadership can be accessible to anyone willing to develop coordination competence through authentic engagement with collective goals.
  • Legitimacy Through Network Service: Bootstrap authority creates legitimacy through service to shared network objectives rather than through external validation or institutional recognition. This form of authority proves particularly resilient because it's grounded in functional competence for advancing goals that stakeholders continue to value, rather than depending on external institutional support that might change. Water board authority persisted through political transformations because it derived from flood management competence that communities continued to need.
  • Democratic Accessibility Without Expert Exclusion: The framework democratizes ethical authority without requiring the abandonment of professional expertise or technical knowledge. Instead, it shows how different types of knowledge and capability can contribute equivalently to collective outcomes. Scientific research enhanced family advocacy; family testimony improved scientific research; legal accountability strengthened both; media coverage amplified all approaches. Professional expertise remains valuable but loses its monopolistic character when coordination enables diverse contributions to shared objectives.
  • Recursive Authority Development: Bootstrap authority creates positive feedback loops where coordination competence enables greater contribution to shared objectives, which increases authority, which enables more effective coordination. This recursive process explains why network-goal coordination often strengthens over time rather than fragmenting, and why it can challenge established institutional authority through sustained effectiveness rather than direct confrontation.
